To analyze the movie character networks, I imported the co-appearance data for 20 films, treating each character as a node and their shared scenes as weighted edges. I then calculated the density (\(\hat{p}\)) of each real network to establish a baseline for the Erdos-Rényi (ER) model, which assumes that every relationship forms with equal, independent probability. By simulating these random “null” graphs, we compared the observed structural metrics, specifically transitivity and modularity, against the values expected by pure chance to determine if cinematic social structures follow a random or intentional design.

Analysis of ER Model

The data reveals a massive clustering gap between the real observed movies and my simulation. In the actual films, the Obs_Clustering is consistently two to three times higher than the ER_Clustering. This shows that movie characters don’t just meet at random. They form tight “cliques” where a friend of a friend is almost always your friend too.

My simulation would also theoretically struggle to replicate the Top_Partnership and Max_scenes columns. The one’s in the table are data from the observed data. In a simulation, all characters would have the same number of co-appearances with the same weight.

Spectral Model

Spectral Analysis

Using the same random sample of 20 films, we then applied spectral analysis by treating each character network as a mathematical object and extracting structural properties from its eigenvalues.

Our goal:

To determine if the mathematical fingerprint of a film’s character network reveals something about how the film is structured — without knowing anything about plot or genre?

Our conclusion:

The answer

Yes. Spectral analysis found two distinct structural types : confined ensemble films vs protagonist-driven films.

Finding 1: Smaller casts aren’t always less connected

Leviathan (12 characters) is 7× more cohesive than Blade Runner (25 characters). Network density matters far more than size.

Finding 2: Larger casts are systematically less cohesive

As cast grows, characters can no longer all interact .The outliers above the line (Mohicans, Coraline, King’s Speech) still have have tight narrative focus despite larger casts.

The red trend line shows the negative relationship clearly. Spend time on the outliers — they’re more interesting than the trend itself. Films above the line are “punching above their weight” in cohesion terms.

Finding 3: Two structural types emerge automatically

The two clusters

Cluster 1 — Dense ensembles

Avg 24 nodes · 99 edges · λ₂ = 1.18

  • Reservoir Dogs
  • TRON
  • Leviathan
  • Alien: Resurrection
  • The Last of the Mohicans
  • The King’s Speech

Small casts where nearly every character interacts with nearly every other.

Cluster 2 — Protagonist-driven

Avg 36 nodes · 89 edges · λ₂ = 0.63

  • The Last Samurai
  • Black Rain · Blade Runner
  • The Reader · Thirteen Days
  • The Crying Game · Crank
  • French Kiss · Coraline
    • 5 more

Larger casts organized around a central character. Peripheral characters don’t meet each other.

Potential reasons for density

Why Cluster 1 is so dense

The main setting of each movie: Reservoir Dogs (diner), Leviathan (submarine), Alien: Resurrection (spaceship), The King’s Speech (therapy room).

These are all confined settings which force all characters to interact with each other

Confined settings force all characters to interact with each other and this is exactly what high edge density and high λ₂ capture.

Why Cluster 2 is sparser

Protagonist-driven films introduce many characters who only interact with the hero and not with each other.

This creates a hub-and-spoke structure that spectral analysis detects immediately.

Summary

Here are our main 3 takeaways from the spectral analysis of the random sample of 20 movie character networks.

1 — All networks are connected, but not equally λ₂ ranges from 0.33 to 2.39 which is a a 7× spread driven by setting and narrative style, not just cast size.

2 — Size and density trade off Larger casts consistently produce sparser networks. Films that break this trend have tight, focused narratives that keep all characters linked.

3 — Two structural archetypes exist Spectral clustering automatically finds a divide between dense ensemble films (confined and protagonist-driven films This matches real narrative structure and this can be discovered with no genre labels.
